# Research Memo: Tri-Valley Unified Staff Survey Analysis

## Executive Summary

We analyzed 500 staff survey responses about Tri-Valley Unified's new communications system installation. Six rounds of iterative investigation — testing hypotheses, challenging findings, and reading actual responses — revealed that **staff generally like the new system itself, but have significant concerns about the rollout process, uneven communication, and localized technical problems.**

The top findings, in plain language:

1. **The system works; the process didn't.** Staff are positive about the technology (audio quality, interface, emergency features) but negative about how the change was managed — poor communication, rushed timelines, and insufficient consultation.

2. **How long someone has been in the district is the single strongest predictor of satisfaction** — and it's not about age or resistance to technology. It's about institutional expectations: long-tenured staff expected to be consulted and feel disrespected when they weren't.

3. **Classified staff were left out of the communication chain.** Custodial, food service, and paraprofessional staff were systematically less informed than teachers and administrators. Zero percent of custodial and food service staff described the communication as adequate.

4. **The North Wing at Hillcrest Elementary has a physical installation defect.** Every single one of the 30 staff members assigned there reports audio problems — garbled sound, dead speakers, inability to hear announcements. This is a facilities issue requiring immediate investigation, not a morale problem.

5. **Satisfaction is relative.** Staff who transferred from districts with worse systems are overwhelmingly positive (87% positive, 0% negative). Staff who transferred from better systems are among the most critical. The same system gets opposite reactions depending on what people are comparing it to.

6. **Valley High School has a broad-based satisfaction deficit** — not concentrated in any one group, but across nearly every position. Communication failures and system reliability complaints drive the gap.

7. **Averages hide dramatic divisions.** Riverside Elementary has the highest average satisfaction — and also contains every one of the 10 most negative responses in the entire dataset. The "best" site is actually the most polarized.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Staff Like the Product, Not the Process

**Evidence:** Question 4 (system functionality) is positive at every site and in every position group, with scores ranging from +0.27 to +0.63 on a -1 to +1 scale. Question 5 (open feedback) is negative at every site — when given a free prompt, staff surface frustrations that structured questions don't capture.

> "The audio quality in my room is outstanding. No complaints about the technology itself." — Teacher, Valley High, 20 years

> "The whole rollout felt rushed and poorly communicated. I would have appreciated being consulted." — Teacher, Riverside Elementary, 5 years

**Why this matters:** The district made a good technology choice. The implementation — not the product — is what generated dissatisfaction. Future technology rollouts should invest proportionally more in change management.

### Finding 2: District Tenure Predicts Satisfaction (Age Does Not)

**Evidence:** Sentiment declines monotonically with years at the district:
- 0-3 years: +0.306 (n=108)
- 4-10 years: +0.194 (n=184)
- 11-20 years: +0.108 (n=132)
- 20+ years: -0.113 (n=76)

The correlation (r=-0.24) holds across all three sites and all eight position types. But age shows essentially zero correlation (r=+0.057). A 55-year-old who is new to the district is just as positive as a 25-year-old newcomer. What matters is how long someone has been part of this institution.

Critically, it's **district tenure, not career length**, that predicts satisfaction. After controlling for years at the district, total years in the profession has zero additional predictive power (partial r=0.01).

> "After 24 years at this school, you'd think someone would loop us in before just ripping out the old system. That's not communication, that's a memo." — Teacher, Hillcrest, 24 years

> "This is probably the smoothest technology transition I've experienced in my 27 years here." — Teacher, Hillcrest, 27 years (positive outlier)

**Why this matters:** The tenure effect is about institutional attachment and expectations of consultation, not technophobia. Long-tenured staff who were positive tended to frame the change in the context of past transitions. Those who were negative felt their institutional knowledge was disregarded.

### Finding 3: Communication Failed Along the Organizational Hierarchy

**Evidence:** 15% of all staff used negative communication language in Q3. But the rate varies dramatically by position:
- Site Administrators: 5% negative, 15% positive
- Teachers: 18% negative, 14% positive
- Paraprofessionals: top complaint was "surprised" (6.5%)
- Custodial/Facilities: 0% positive communication language
- Food Service: 0% positive communication language

> "I had no idea this was happening until the installers showed up. Nobody told the aides anything." — Paraprofessional, Riverside, 34 years

> "They told us it was coming. That was about it." — Custodial staff member

**Why this matters:** Information flowed down the hierarchy and stopped before reaching classified staff. This isn't just a morale issue — staff who aren't informed can't prepare, leading to operational disruptions (food service bell schedule chaos, custodial workflow conflicts).

### Finding 4: North Wing at Hillcrest Has a Physical Installation Defect

**Evidence:** All 30 North Wing staff members mention audio problems — garbled sound, muffled speakers, dead air, inability to hear announcements. This is 100% prevalence, across every position type and tenure band. North Wing demographics are not unusual compared to the rest of Hillcrest. The issue is purely physical.

Paraprofessionals (n=5, mean -0.360) and counselors are hit hardest. Counselors specifically cite intercom interruptions during sensitive student sessions.

> "I've been here 18 years and this is the worst the intercom has ever sounded in my area. Half the time there's just dead air or garbled noise." — Paraprofessional, Hillcrest North Wing

> "You can't make out what anyone's saying. It's garbled and way too quiet." — Staff member, Hillcrest North Wing

**Why this matters:** This is the most immediately actionable finding. A facilities team should inspect the North Wing speaker installation. This problem is depressing Hillcrest's overall satisfaction scores and affecting 30 staff members daily.

### Finding 5: Prior Experience Sets the Reference Frame

**Evidence:** Among the 68 staff who transferred from other districts:
- From districts with **worse** systems (n=30): mean +0.582, 87% positive, **0% negative**
- From **comparable** systems (n=12): mean +0.215, 42% positive
- From **better** systems (n=26): mean +0.102, 35% positive, 39% negative

The 0.48-point gap between "worse" and "better" origin groups is the largest single-variable effect in the dataset. This is a reference-frame effect: satisfaction reflects expectations set by prior experience.

> "Coming from a district where the communications setup was really outdated, I appreciate how much more capable this one is." — Teacher, Riverside, transfer from worse system

> "At my last district everything just worked. You picked up the handset, pressed a button, and it was done. This takes more fiddling." — Office Staff, Riverside, transfer from better system

**Why this matters:** The same system can generate opposite reactions. When evaluating satisfaction data, knowing what people are comparing against is essential context.

### Finding 6: Valley High Has a Broad-Based Satisfaction Deficit

**Evidence:** Valley High scores lowest on overall sentiment (+0.095 vs +0.201 Riverside, +0.166 Hillcrest). The deficit appears in 6 of 8 position types and persists after removing the most negative subgroups. Valley High teachers (n=96) cite communication failures, system reliability issues, and lost instructional time.

Notably, Valley High actually **leads** on Q2 (training satisfaction: +0.200). Its weakness is Q1 (overall experience: -0.120) and Q5 (open feedback: -0.150).

One bright spot: staff in Large/Specialized rooms (gyms, theaters) at Valley High are very positive (+0.370), suggesting the system performs well in large spaces.

> "I came back from winter break and there was new equipment in my classroom. Nobody told me it was happening." — Teacher, Valley High, 20 years

> "The timing inconsistencies with bells and announcements have thrown off my lesson pacing more days than not." — Teacher, Valley High

**Why this matters:** Valley High's problems are not about one disgruntled group — they reflect a site-wide experience of poor communication and reliability. The training was adequate; the rollout was not.

### Finding 7: Food Service Staff Were Operationally Harmed

**Evidence:** Food Service is the only position group with negative mean sentiment (-0.032). They report bell schedule changes that disrupted lunch service operations at every site.

They are also among the district's "quiet dissenters" — 29% use hedging language ("it's fine", "good enough"), nearly twice the baseline rate. Their actual dissatisfaction is likely understated by standard scoring.

> "The bell schedule kept changing during the install and it threw off our whole lunch service. We had kids showing up early, showing up late, nobody knew what was going on." — Food Service, Valley High

> "During the install our lunch periods got moved around three different times in two weeks." — Food Service, Riverside, 29 years

**Why this matters:** Bell schedule changes during installation had direct operational impact on food preparation, student nutrition logistics, and kitchen staffing. This is a concrete harm, not just a sentiment issue.

### Finding 8: Counselors Value Safety Features — But Face Confidentiality Risks

**Evidence:** 92% of counselors/specialists mention emergency alert and safety features (vs. 25% of teachers, <10% of other positions). Long-tenured counselors (20+ years) are an exception to the tenure-negativity pattern: they remain positive (+0.326 mean) because they weight safety improvements over workflow disruption.

However, counselors also report that intercom announcements interrupt sensitive student sessions, describing genuine harm to student welfare.

> "The emergency features are what I value most. Knowing the system is reliable in a crisis gives me real peace of mind." — Counselor, Valley High, 25 years

> "I've had students completely shut down after an interruption." — Counselor, describing intercom during counseling sessions

**Why this matters:** Counselors see the system through a safety lens that other positions don't share. Their call for a "do not disturb" mode during counseling sessions addresses a real student welfare concern.

## Surprises

1. **Age doesn't matter; district tenure does.** The conventional assumption that "older staff resist change" is wrong in this data. A 60-year-old who is new to the district is among the most positive respondents. What creates resistance is accumulated institutional experience and unmet expectations of consultation.

2. **Valley High leads on training despite trailing on everything else.** The site with the lowest overall satisfaction actually has the best-rated training. Valley High's problem isn't knowledge gaps — it's communication failures and system reliability.

3. **Riverside is the most polarized site, not Valley High.** Despite having the highest average satisfaction, Riverside contains all 10 of the most negative responses in the entire dataset. The "best" site masks the worst individual experiences.

4. **The gender gap flips by site.** Males are dramatically more negative at Valley High (-0.154) but actually more positive than females at Riverside (+0.253). Controlling for position doesn't explain this — something site-specific is driving it. Male custodial staff rate +0.800 at Riverside but -0.497 at Valley High — a 1.3-point swing in the same job.

5. **Race differences are invisible in the aggregate but large among veterans.** Overall, race/ethnicity shows modest differences. But among 20+ year staff, Hispanic/Latino veterans are strongly positive (+0.253) while White veterans are the most negative group in the dataset (-0.231). These opposite effects cancel out in averages.

6. **Quiet dissenters are hiding in the numbers.** About 18% of respondents use hedging language ("it's fine", "good enough", "I guess") that signals resigned acceptance rather than genuine satisfaction. Custodial and food service staff hedge at nearly twice the baseline rate. Reclassifying these respondents shifts the district's negative response rate from 29% to 34%.

## Limitations

- **Keyword-based sentiment scoring** is approximate. It misses negation (26.8% of records), hedging (17.6%), and sarcasm. It was validated against a context-aware assessment (95% agreement, no direction flips), but nuance is lost. Key findings held up under scrutiny but the exact numbers should be treated as estimates, not precise measurements.

- **Small subgroup sizes** limit many comparisons. District Office Staff (n=12), Site Administrators (n=20), North Wing transfers (n=4), and many position-within-site cells drop to single digits. Patterns in these groups are suggestive, not conclusive.

- **Correlation, not causation.** Tenure correlates with sentiment, but also with position type, building assignment, and many other variables. We cannot isolate a single causal mechanism from survey data alone.

- **Single point in time.** These responses capture the post-installation period. Staff who are negative now may become positive as the system stabilizes, or vice versa.

- **Self-reported data from an anonymous survey.** Site administrators appear to speak in "official voice" even in anonymous surveys, potentially understating their concerns. Other groups may over- or under-report for various reasons.

- **No student or parent perspectives.** The impact on students (instructional time loss, counseling disruptions) is reported by staff, not measured directly.

## Recommended Follow-up

1. **Dispatch a facilities team to Hillcrest North Wing immediately.** All 30 staff report audio problems. This is a physical installation defect with a physical fix. Every day it persists, 30 staff members have a degraded experience that depresses satisfaction scores site-wide.

2. **Investigate Valley High system reliability.** Teachers report glitches, cutouts, and inconsistent bell timing. Determine whether these are installation issues (fixable) or inherent system limitations (requiring vendor engagement).

3. **Add a "do not disturb" mode for counseling offices.** Counselors broadly support the system but describe real harm when announcements interrupt sensitive student sessions. This is a configuration change, not a hardware issue.

4. **Conduct targeted listening sessions with classified staff.** Custodial, food service, and paraprofessional staff were systematically left out of the communication chain. A follow-up conversation — not another survey — would acknowledge their experience and surface specific operational concerns.

5. **Brief long-tenured staff before the next technology change.** The tenure-sentiment pattern is driven by unmet expectations of consultation. Including veteran staff early in the planning process for future changes would likely shift their experience significantly.

6. **Re-examine the bell schedule change process.** Food service staff at every site reported operational disruption from bell schedule changes during installation. Future installations should coordinate with food service before altering bell timing.

7. **Investigate the Valley High gender gap.** Males at Valley High are significantly more negative than females across nearly every position, and this pattern does not appear at other sites. A human researcher should explore whether there are site-specific dynamics (communication style, department culture, building layout) driving this.

8. **Don't rely on site averages.** Riverside's high average masks extreme internal divisions. Any follow-up should look at distributions, not just means — and should specifically compare experiences across positions within the same site.
